INTRODUCTION:

We can get so deeply entrenched in our own worldview that it is difficult to see beyond it. We already have plenty of ideas about what the self might look like, but its hard to see a new perspective unless you go out and ask. So Cory took to the streets in search of new perspectives from strangers. He talked to three people, a Navajo flute player, a mother with her children who we will call "Alice", and a lady on a park bench who said she was terminally ill and trying to get home. We think we have learned a great deal from them, and hopefully you will too.

In the interests of creating a more robust dialogue, we will begin by considering our notions of self at the beginning of this experiment. Both Jason and Cory come from quite similar backgrounds, we are both white males who are in their senior year at CU Boulder. We have been exposed to some controversial ideas about self-hood through the ideas of thinkers whose works we have read and watched in this class. One of the primary partitions in our understanding of self lies along the dimension of free-will vs. fate. Even in the Western tradition that we grew up in, there is a great deal of contention concerning the choices we make. Do we make them of our own will, or are these choices made for us? A scientific perspective might favor the idea of fate - that every action has its reaction and we are all masses of particles being acted upon by other particles. In this context, free-will seems to violate causality. But the idea that we are not responsible for our own actions is difficult to stomach. What can our lives mean if we have no choice? What does justice mean if no-one can even choose? Our interviewees gave us some interesting perspectives on this pivotal question.

REFLECTION:

Neither Cody nor Jason have any experience of oppression that compares with that of Leaf-In-The-Wind, nor do we have children and families of our own to understand the perspective of "Alice", and we still have many years in our life which makes Cheryl's perspective distant from our own. The position that someone holds in life will do a great deal to shape that person's idea of themselves. Alice seemed to be in favor of the notion that destiny holds sway over us. She spoke about the effect of her family on herself - it was difficult to even define herself outside the context of that family. Likewise for us.
Cory and myself may not be married or have children, but our parents and siblings and extended family have been the driving factor in the development of our characters. And we had no say in any of that. Consider Leaf-In-The-Wind - he has been imprisoned in one way or another for most of his life, and yet he still holds on to his heritage above anything else. Even Cheryl, who was the greatest advocate of free-will acknowledged the possibility that our genes (which of course come from our parents) play a role in how we relate to the situations that we confront.

In the end we are probably not any closer to knowing the "truth" about the nature of the self, but we have been left with ideas to consider. If anything was clear to us during our interviews, it was that ideas do a great deal to shape the reality that you experience and create.
